## 1. Vocation

*What CTR is called to be. The call it is responding to.*

Christ the Reconciler is a missional community of reconciliation made up of Protestants, Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, Pentecostals, and Jews — both residential and non-residential members — seeking to live out John 17, Jesus' only recorded prayer for all his people.

The word *ecumenical* describes both the composition and the goal of the community. CTR is ecumenical in who is in the room, and ecumenical in what it is trying to produce.

CTR is a community of **lay followers of Jesus** — not a religious order, not a denomination, not a parachurch program. Its members are rooted in their own churches, parishes, and synagogues. The community does not replace those roots. It deepens them.

CTR is not an end in itself. The community exists to **send people back**. Members — whether they live on the land in Elgin or participate from a distance — are formed by the common life and returned to their own parishes, churches, congregations, and synagogues as agents of reconciliation. What happens at CTR is meant to ripple outward into every ecclesial context its members inhabit.

The vision for the physical land in Elgin captures this well: CTR is meant to be **a visible and visitable example of lived reconciliation** — a place people can come to, experience, and carry back with them.

The vocation is grounded in Jesus' prayer the night before his crucifixion:

> "That they all may be one, Father, just as you are in me and I am in you — may they also be one in us, so that the world will believe that you sent me."
> — John 17:21

Jesus connects the unity of his people directly to the intelligibility of the gospel. A divided and hostile church obscures the witness of Jesus to the watching world. A reconciled church makes that witness visible. CTR exists in service of that visibility.

By God's grace, CTR has been able to impact, face to face, people from 54 nations — more than 25% of the nations on earth.

## 2. Charism

*The particular grace God has entrusted to this community for the building up of the Body of Christ.*

The word *charism* shares its root with *charity* and *grace* — it refers to a particular gift of the Holy Spirit given not for the recipient's benefit alone, but for the building up of the whole Body of Christ. Charisms are always specific: given to particular people, in particular relationships, for a particular moment in the life of the Church.

CTR's charism is **reconciliation across genuine Christian difference** — not as a program or a method, but as a grace received and embodied in the particularity of its members' lives and relationships.

This charism was first embodied in the founders. Thomas and Amy Cogdell are a Protestant-Catholic married couple who have had to practice reconciliation not as a theory but as a daily reality — across the dinner table, in how they pray, in how they raise their children, in how they navigate the real and unresolved differences between their traditions. Their marriage is not an illustration of the charism. It *is* the charism, at its most intimate scale.

From that marriage, the grace has extended outward — to a household, to a community, to a network of relationships across the Christian world. The same grace that operates between a husband and wife operates between traditions: it requires honesty about real difference, renunciation of hostility, concrete acts of repair, and patient restoration of relationship.

CTR's charism is therefore not generic ecumenism — a polite setting-aside of differences in favor of what everyone shares. It is the harder and more specific grace of **remaining in genuine relationship across genuine difference**, allowing that relationship to change you, and offering what you have learned to the Church.

## 3. Rule of Life

*The spiritual pattern that makes common life possible. The deep grammar of commitment.*

CTR's Rule of Life is the **Statement of Personal Devotion to Live Out John 17**. It is a personal covenant, made freely, by individuals who want to take Jesus' prayer seriously in their own lives.

The Statement is not a statement of intent. It is a description of how you will actually act.

It contains:

**A foundation of faith** — affirmation of the historic Christian faith and active membership in one's own local church, parish, congregation, or synagogue. The Rule assumes you are already rooted somewhere. It does not replace that rootedness; it deepens it.

**The Five Renunciations** — concrete, named renunciations of the specific hostilities that divide the Body of Christ: hostility toward others, anti-Semitism, anti-Catholicism, anti-Protestantism, and contempt toward all whom Christ has accepted. These are specific because hostility is specific. Vague commitments to be nicer do not address the actual wounds.

**The Five Commitments** — concrete actions: praying for unity, being a minister of reconciliation, grieving the divisions of the church, thanking God for what he is doing in other traditions, and honoring other traditions' communion practices while remaining true to one's own.

**A request for power** — because none of this is possible in human strength alone.

The Statement is renewed annually by non-residential members, typically each February. For residential members, it is a living commitment woven into the rhythms of daily life.

Anyone, anywhere, from any tradition, can make this commitment. It does not require moving to Elgin.

## 4. Common Life

*The concrete rhythms by which members and participants share prayer, work, meals, formation, and friendship.*

If the Rule of Life is the grammar, Common Life is the language actually spoken. It is what people experience when they show up — whether they live on the land or participate from a distance.

### The Five Practices

CTR's common life is organized around five practices. These are not a program or curriculum. They are the outward shape of a community formed by John 17.

**Prayer** — The community gathers daily around the Bridge Prayer, a short liturgy that joins its members to Christians across traditions and around the world. Prayer is the first practice because reconciliation begins with standing before God together before it can happen between people.

**Reconciliation** — Reconciliation is a concrete set of actions that removes hostility in a relationship, repairs the damage it caused, and restores the God-intended unity. The community practices it with one another and teaches others how. CTR's particular focus is *ecclesial* reconciliation — between Christian traditions — while recognizing that this form of reconciliation is intimately connected to, and cannot be fully separated from, other forms: racial, marital, communal. The grace is one grace operating at different scales.

**Formation** — The community is shaped by scripture, teaching, and the long tradition of the Church. Formation is the practice of allowing yourself to be changed — by what you read, what you hear, and who you do life with. This includes the Teaching of the Week, the Book of the Month, the podcast library, and the retreats.

**Hospitality** — CTR's door is open. Hospitality is the practice of welcoming the stranger, sharing the table, and making room — for guests, retreatants, and anyone who wants to come closer. Retreats are a primary expression of this practice and are open to anyone.

**Work** — Life together requires real work: cooking, building, gardening, administration. Work is not a distraction from the mission. It is part of it. The practice of work is the practice of showing up faithfully for what needs to be done — and finding, in that faithfulness, a form of reconciliation that does not announce itself.

### The Physical Place

The common life is rooted in a specific place: 16.444 acres in Elgin, Texas, thirty minutes east of Austin.

The land is being developed in phases, with the vision of becoming a visible and visitable example of lived reconciliation:

**What exists now** — The ministry center (the original house, finished in 2014) serves as the hub of community life: prayer, meetings, hospitality, and retreats. The Cielo warehouse (9,600 sq ft, completed in Phase 2) houses the bottled water business that is the economic engine of the community.

**Cielo** — Cielo is CTR's bottled water company. It is not a side project or a fundraising device. It is a genuine business whose purpose is to sustain the mission — a concrete expression of the practice of work. ### Levels of Participation

Common life at CTR is not one-size-fits-all. There are multiple ways to participate:

**Residential members** — those living on the land in Elgin, sharing daily prayer, meals, and work.

**Non-residential community members** — those committed to the Rule of Life and the common life from a distance, rooted in their own ecclesial contexts, returning regularly. CTR has non-residential members in other parts of the US and in Germany.

**Friends, supporters, and distant participants** — those who follow along, pray the Bridge Prayer, listen to teachings, and support the work. This is not a lesser form of participation. It is essential.

## 5. Mission

*How the community's common life is offered outwardly for the Church and the world.*

CTR's mission flows directly from its vocation. If the vocation is to embody and practice reconciliation, the mission is to offer that embodiment outward — as witness, as teaching, as service, and as sending.

### Sending

The primary missionary movement of CTR is **sending people back**. Members formed by the common life return to their own parishes, churches, congregations, and synagogues as agents of reconciliation. The community does not seek to grow by accumulation. It seeks to grow by multiplication — one reconciled person, returning to one ecclesial context, changing it from within.

### Teaching and Resources

CTR offers its teachings, podcasts, written reflections, and retreats to the wider Church. These are not merely content. They are an extension of the common life — an invitation to participate in the practices and the Rule from wherever you are.

The teaching library includes:

- **Podcast series** organized by theme (Foundations of Reconciliation, Boundaries of Reconciliation, Foundations of Community, The Nicene Creed) and by teacher (Thomas Cogdell, Amy Cogdell, George Miley, Marianna Gol, Mark Kinzer, Peter Hocken, Phillip and Caroline Owens, Richard Harvey, Sandi Pedrotti, and others)
- **The Wittenberg 2017 archive** — recordings from the five gatherings, a historical resource of significant ecumenical weight
- **Passing Through Baca** — Amy Cogdell's written reflections
- **Reconciliation Stories** — narratives of reconciliation in practice

### The Theological Foundation

The urgency of CTR's mission rests on a theological claim: that the unity of Christians is not merely a nice internal goal, but **a constitutive element of the gospel's intelligibility to the watching world**.

Jesus said it himself: *"so that the world will believe that you sent me."* The Edinburgh Missionary Conference of 1910 — the founding moment of the modern ecumenical movement — recognized this connection: a divided church cannot credibly proclaim a reconciling God. What CTR is doing is not peripheral to evangelism. It is essential to it.

## Key Themes to Carry Through All Copy

**Sending, not just gathering.** CTR exists to form and send, not to accumulate. Every page should imply that the point is to go back changed, not to stay.

**Concrete, not aspirational.** Reconciliation is actions, not feelings. The Rule is not intentions, it is behaviors. Formation is not inspiration, it is change. Keep the language specific and active.

**Genuine difference, not least-common-denominator.** CTR is not about finding what everyone agrees on and stopping there. It is about remaining in real relationship across real difference. The differences are not dissolved. The hostility is.

**The watching world.** The stakes are not merely internal to the church. A reconciled church makes the gospel visible. A hostile church obscures it. This connection should appear on the homepage, the mission page, and anywhere the *why* of CTR is being explained.

**Ecumenical in composition and goal.** CTR is made up of Protestants, Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, Pentecostals, and Jews — and it exists to produce more of that kind of relationship in the world. Both halves of that sentence matter.

**The marriage at the center.** Thomas and Amy's Protestant-Catholic marriage is not a biographical detail. It is the charism made flesh. It should appear early in the About section and be referenced as the origin of the community's grace.

**Lay community.** CTR is a community of lay followers of Jesus. This is an important ecclesiological signal — it distinguishes CTR from a religious order while still using intentional community language.

**The land speaks.** The physical space in Elgin is not incidental. It is a theological statement — a visible and visitable example of lived reconciliation. The architecture orients toward prayer. The cemetery signals permanence. The bridge represents the work. Use the land as a way of making the mission concrete and tangible.

**Scale and reach.** 54 nations, face to face. Members in Texas, across the US, and in Germany. Wittenberg 2017 brought together Christians from across Europe and beyond. CTR is small in size and large in reach — this should be communicated without boasting.
