%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%
### Formation

Reconciliation requires people who have been changed. Not just people who have agreed to try harder, or who have signed a statement of good intentions — but people who have actually been formed into something different than what they were.

Formation is the practice of allowing that change to happen.

---

%%SECTION: WHAT FORMATION IS%%
### What Formation Is

Formation is not education. Education transfers information. Formation changes the person receiving it.

At CTR, formation happens through scripture, through the long tradition of the Church, through teaching, through the company you keep, and through the daily friction of living across genuine difference with people who will not let you stay comfortable in your assumptions.

That last one is underrated. Living in community with people from other traditions — really living with them, not just attending conferences together — is one of the most powerful forces of formation there is. You cannot maintain a caricature of Catholics if you are sharing meals with Catholics every day. You cannot hold onto a dismissive view of Evangelicals if an Evangelical is your neighbor and your friend. The common life forms you whether you intend it to or not.

Formation at CTR is the practice of being intentional about that process — choosing the inputs that shape you, and submitting yourself to the community that will hold you accountable for what you are becoming.

%%SECTION: FORMATION AND THE TRADITION%%
### Formation and the Whole Tradition

One of the gifts of an ecumenical community is access to the whole tradition of the Church — not just the slice of it that your own tradition has preserved.

Catholics bring liturgy, the sacraments, the contemplative tradition, the richness of the Church Fathers. Protestants bring the primacy of scripture, the theology of grace, the tradition of preaching. Orthodox Christians bring theosis, the icon tradition, the deep patristic inheritance. Messianic Jews bring the Hebrew roots of the faith, the feasts, the unbroken thread connecting the Church to its Jewish origins. Pentecostals bring the immediate presence and power of the Holy Spirit.

None of these traditions has the whole picture. All of them have something the others need. Formation at CTR means allowing yourself to receive from traditions other than your own — not abandoning your own, but letting it be enriched.

This is not theological relativism. CTR holds firm to the ancient Christian faith. But within that faith, there is far more richness than any single tradition has fully received. Formation is the practice of reaching for that richness.

%%SECTION: FORMATION AND HUMILITY%%
### Formation and Humility

CTR's core values include what the community calls "living in humility" — defined not as self-deprecation but as living according to the truth. The truth is that we are beloved of God and broken by our own choices. Both things are true at the same time.

Formation is the practice of holding both — receiving God's love without losing sight of the ways we still need to change. It is the willingness to be wrong about things you were certain of. It is the openness to having your mind changed by a tradition you once dismissed.

This kind of humility is not weakness. It is the precondition of reconciliation. You cannot be reconciled to someone you are not willing to learn from.
