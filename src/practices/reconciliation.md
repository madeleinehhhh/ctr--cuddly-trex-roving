%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%
### Reconciliation

Reconciliation is not a feeling. It is not a posture. It is not a metaphor.

Reconciliation is a concrete set of actions that removes hostility in a relationship, repairs the damage it caused, and restores the God-intended unity between the people involved.

It can be done. It must be done. And it begins with deciding that hostility is not acceptable.

---

%%SECTION: WHAT RECONCILIATION IS%%
### What Reconciliation Actually Is

The word gets used so often, and so loosely, that it has lost its edges. People speak of reconciliation when they mean tolerance, or coexistence, or simply not fighting anymore. CTR means something more specific.

Reconciliation has three movements:

**Removing hostility** — Hostility is not just open conflict. It is contempt, dismissiveness, the assumption that the other is beneath engagement. It lives in how we talk about each other from the pulpit, in the words we choose on social media, in the tone we use when someone from another tradition is mentioned. Removing hostility means naming it, renouncing it, and stopping it — concretely, in specific relationships and specific contexts.

**Repairing damage** — Hostility causes real wounds. The Catholic-Protestant divide carries centuries of violence, condescension, and mutual excommunication. The wounds between Christians and Jews run even deeper. Repair does not pretend these wounds didn't happen. It acknowledges them, grieves them, and does what can be done to make them right. This is the work of repentance — not as a feeling, but as an action.

**Restoring unity** — The goal is not neutrality or peaceful coexistence. The goal is the unity Jesus prayed for — an active, living, mutual relationship between people who are genuinely different and genuinely one. Not uniform. One.

%%SECTION: ECCLESIAL RECONCILIATION%%
### Ecclesial Reconciliation: CTR's Particular Focus

There are many forms of reconciliation — racial, marital, communal, political. CTR does not claim these are unrelated to each other. The grace of reconciliation is one grace operating at different scales, and many CTR members are involved in forms of reconciliation that go well beyond the ecclesial.

But CTR's particular calling — its specific focus — is **ecclesial reconciliation**: reconciliation between Christian traditions. Between Catholics and Protestants. Between Christians and Messianic Jews. Between Orthodox and Evangelical. Between communities that have treated each other as enemies for generations.

This focus is not arbitrary. It flows directly from John 17. Jesus prayed for the unity of his people — his *ecclesial* people, the Church he was building. And he connected that unity directly to the credibility of the gospel: *"so that the world will believe that you sent me."* The divisions of the Church are not merely sad. They are a wound in the witness of Jesus to the watching world.

Healing that wound is the work.

%%SECTION: RECONCILIATION AS A WAY OF LIFE%%
### Reconciliation as a Way of Life

Reconciliation is not a project with a completion date. It is a way of life — a set of habits, postures, and practices that you bring to every relationship, every conversation, every ecclesial encounter.

At CTR, this means the community is constantly practicing reconciliation with one another — not just preaching it. A Protestant and a Catholic who disagree about the Eucharist still share a meal. An Orthodox Christian and an Evangelical who hold different views on salvation still pray together. The disagreements do not disappear. The hostility does.

This is harder than it sounds. The Five Renunciations in the Statement of Personal Devotion exist because the temptations are real: to slip back into contempt, to allow dismissiveness to creep in, to treat difference as threat. The practice of reconciliation is the daily choice not to do that.

It is also, over time, a source of deep joy. The community at CTR will tell you that living across genuine difference — really living it, not just tolerating it — produces a richness that a homogeneous community cannot. You learn things about God, and about the faith, that you could not learn any other way.

%%SECTION: RECONCILIATION AND THE WATCHING WORLD%%
### The Watching World

Jesus did not pray for unity because it would make his followers feel better. He prayed for it because the watching world would take note.

*"So that the world will believe that you sent me."*

A divided and hostile church cannot credibly proclaim a reconciling God. When Christians treat each other with contempt, the world sees it and draws the obvious conclusion: whatever these people have, it isn't working.

When Christians from genuinely different traditions remain in genuine relationship — not despite their differences but through them — the world sees something it cannot easily explain. It sees a reconciliation that did not come from human effort alone. And that witness opens a door that argument cannot.

CTR's reconciliation work is not peripheral to evangelism. It is essential to it. This is the insight the Edinburgh Missionary Conference of 1910 recognized at the birth of the modern ecumenical movement — and it is as true now as it was then.
