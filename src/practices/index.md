%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%
### A Rule and a Way of Life

Jesus, the night before he died, prayed one prayer for all his people — that we would be one. Not someday. Not metaphorically. One.

At Christ the Reconciler, everything we do is a response to that prayer. Over time, that response has taken two concrete shapes: a **rule** and a **way of life**.

Both flow from John 17. Both are for anyone who wants to take Jesus' prayer seriously — whether you live here, follow from a distance, or are just beginning to explore.

%%SECTION: THE RULE%%
### The Rule: A Statement of Personal Devotion

A rule of life is an ancient practice — a structured set of commitments that keeps you accountable to what you say you believe, and shapes how you actually treat people.

The Statement of Personal Devotion is CTR's rule. It is a personal covenant, made freely, by individuals who want to concretely live out John 17 in their own lives. It includes five renunciations — things you leave behind — and five commitments — things you take up. These are not aspirations. They are actions.

You don't have to live in Elgin to make this commitment. The Statement is for anyone — wherever you are, whatever tradition you come from — who wants to say yes to John 17.

%%SECTION: THE WAY OF LIFE%%
### The Way of Life: Five Practices

A way of life is less formal than a rule — a set of rhythms and orientations that shape you over time. You grow into a way of life. You practice it imperfectly. You return to it when you've drifted.

CTR's way of life is organized around five practices. These are not a program or a curriculum. They are the outward expression of what it looks like to be a community — and a person — shaped by John 17.

**[[ctr-practices-prayer|Prayer →]]**
We pray daily, together and apart, for the unity of the Body of Christ. The Bridge Prayer is the heartbeat of this practice — a short daily liturgy that joins us to Christians across traditions and around the world.

**[[ctr-practices-reconciliation|Reconciliation →]]**
Reconciliation is not a feeling. It is a concrete set of actions that removes hostility, repairs damage, and restores the God-intended unity. We practice it with each other. We teach others how.

**[[ctr-practices-formation|Formation →]]**
We are shaped by scripture, by teaching, and by the long tradition of the church. Formation is the practice of letting yourself be changed — by what you read, what you hear, and who you do life with.

**[[ctr-practices-hospitality|Hospitality →]]**
Our door is open. Hospitality is the practice of welcoming the stranger, sharing the table, and making room — for guests, for retreatants, for anyone who wants to come closer.

**[[ctr-practices-work|Work →]]**
Life together requires real work. Cooking, building, gardening, administration — work is not a distraction from our mission. It is part of it.
