%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%
### John 17: The Root of Everything

On the night before his crucifixion, Jesus gathered his disciples and prayed. It is the longest prayer of Jesus recorded in the Gospels. And in the heart of that prayer, he turned his attention to everyone who would ever believe in him — including us — and asked his Father for one thing:

> *"That they all may be one, Father, just as you are in me and I am in you — may they also be one in us, so that the world will believe that you sent me."*
> — John 17:21

This is the prayer that gave birth to Christ the Reconciler.

---

%%SECTION: WHAT THE PRAYER MEANS%%
### Not Uniformity. Unity.

Jesus was not praying for a world without theological difference. He was praying for something far more radical — that his people would be united in the same way the Father and the Son are united. A unity of love, of mutual honor, of genuine relationship across real difference.

The bar he set is staggering. And he set it knowing exactly what the church would look like two thousand years later — fractured, hostile, suspicious, divided into thousands of competing traditions. He prayed this prayer anyway.

We believe that prayer has never been withdrawn. It still stands. And we believe it can be answered — not by everyone agreeing on everything, but by Christians choosing, concretely and deliberately, to end the hostility.

---

%%SECTION: WHY IT MATTERS%%
### What's at Stake

Jesus connects the unity of his people directly to the witness of the gospel: *"so that the world will believe that you sent me."* The divisions and hostilities of the church are not just an internal problem. They are a wound in the witness of Jesus to the watching world.

We are not trying to build a perfect church. We are trying to be a community that takes seriously what Jesus asked for — and demonstrates, however imperfectly, that it is possible.

---

%%SECTION: THE TWO RESPONSES%%
### How We Respond

Over time, our response to John 17 has taken two concrete shapes.

**A rule** — the Statement of Personal Devotion — a personal covenant that anyone can make. It names what you are leaving behind (the Five Renunciations) and what you are taking up (the Five Commitments). It is a formal, structured way of saying yes to John 17 in concrete, visible action.

**A way of life** — five practices that give shape to how we pray, relate, learn, welcome, and work together. Less formal than the rule, but no less serious. A set of rhythms you grow into over time.

Both are for anyone. Neither requires moving to Elgin. Both are an answer to the same prayer.

%%SECTION: INVITATION%%
### An Invitation Into the Trinity

John 17 is not just a prayer about the church. It is an invitation into the life of God. Jesus prays that his people would be one *as* the Father and Son are one — which means the unity he is asking for is not merely organizational or doctrinal. It is participation in the love that flows between the persons of the Trinity, made visible in how Christians actually treat each other.

This is the deepest ground of everything we do. We are not managing conflict. We are not practicing religious diplomacy. We are being drawn into something far larger than ourselves — and it shows.

> *"I in them and you in me — so that they will be brought to complete unity."*
> — John 17:23
