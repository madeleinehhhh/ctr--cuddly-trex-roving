%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%

### Work

Common life requires real work. Cooking, cleaning, building, gardening, administrating, maintaining — the unglamorous, necessary labor that keeps a household running and a community alive.

Work is not a distraction from the mission of Christ the Reconciler. It is part of it.

---

%%SECTION: WHAT WORK IS%%

### What Work Is

It would be easy to treat work as the least theological of the five practices — the practical infrastructure that supports the more obviously spiritual activities of prayer, reconciliation, formation, and hospitality. That would be a mistake.

Work is where reconciliation gets tested at its most ordinary. It is easy to feel united with someone during a moving prayer service or a rich theological conversation. It is harder — and more revealing — to remain in genuine, patient, non-hostile relationship with them when you are both tired, the kitchen needs cleaning, and someone forgot to do their part.

The daily practice of work together is the practice of showing up faithfully for what needs to be done, regardless of how you feel, regardless of who you are working alongside, regardless of whether the work feels meaningful in the moment. That faithfulness is itself a form of reconciliation. It is a choice, made repeatedly, to remain present and engaged with the people and the place God has given you.

%%SECTION: CIELO%%

### Cielo: Work as Economic Engine

The most visible expression of CTR's work practice is Cielo — the community's bottled water company, operated out of a 9,600 sq ft warehouse on the Elgin property.

Cielo is not a fundraising mechanism. It is a genuine business, built and run by community members, whose purpose is to sustain the mission of Christ the Reconciler financially. It is the economic engine that keeps the community running, the retreats possible, and the door open.

In this way, Cielo embodies something important about how CTR understands work: it is not separate from the sacred. The labor of running a business — managing production, maintaining equipment, handling logistics, serving customers — is offered as part of the community's vocation. The work of Cielo is the work of Christ the Reconciler.

%%SECTION: WORK AND THE BENEDICT TRADITION%%

### Work and the Long Tradition

The integration of work into a community's spiritual life is not a new idea. Benedictine monasteries have operated on the principle of *ora et labora* — pray and work — for fifteen centuries. The rhythm of prayer and labor, held together in a common life, has formed countless communities and countless Christians.

CTR is not a monastery. But it shares the conviction that work and prayer are not opposites to be balanced against each other. They are partners. The person who prays faithfully and works faithfully is being formed in ways that neither prayer nor work alone can accomplish.

The maker space planned for Phase 3 of the physical development extends this vision — a shared space for building, creating, and making, open to community members and potentially to the wider neighborhood. Work as creativity. Work as gift. Work as a form of hospitality offered outward.

%%SECTION: WORK FOR NON-RESIDENTIAL MEMBERS%%

### Work Beyond Elgin

Not everyone who participates in CTR's common life lives on the land in Elgin. For non-residential members and distant participants, the practice of work takes a different shape — but it is no less real.

The work practice for those not living at CTR is the practice of bringing the same faithfulness to your own daily work that the community brings to its work in Elgin. Your office, your studio, your classroom, your kitchen — these are the places where your work practice lives. The question the community asks is: can you bring to your daily work the same quality of presence, faithfulness, and non-hostility that you are trying to bring to your relationships across traditions?

Work is where the common life meets the ordinary life. It is where the formation that happens in prayer and reconciliation and hospitality gets tested and proven real.
