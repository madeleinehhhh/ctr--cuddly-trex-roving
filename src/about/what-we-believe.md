%%SECTION: HERO / OPENING%%
### What We Believe

CTR does not hold a lowest-common-denominator faith. Our members hold their own traditions — Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, Messianic Jewish, Pentecostal — seriously, with conviction and love.

What we share is not a reduced set of beliefs everyone can agree on. It is a set of convictions about what it means to live as Christians who are genuinely different from one another and refuse to be enemies.

---

%%SECTION: THE ANCIENT FAITH%%
### The Ancient Faith

The ground we stand on together is the ancient Christian faith — the Trinitarian God, the incarnation, death and resurrection of Jesus Christ, the work of the Holy Spirit, the one holy catholic and apostolic Church. This is the faith of the creeds, shared across Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant traditions for two millennia.

Each CTR community member is an active, committed member of their own church, parish, congregation, or synagogue. We do not ask anyone to leave their tradition. We do not create a new denomination. The ancient faith is not a lowest common denominator — it is the deep well from which every tradition in the community drinks.

%%SECTION: CORE VALUES%%
### Core Values

Beyond the shared ground of the ancient faith, CTR holds a set of values it believes are essential for walking out the ministry of reconciliation. These are not universal Christian obligations — they are the particular convictions that shape this community's specific calling.

**Knowing the Love of God**
It is necessary to assent to the truth that we are loved by God. But it is not enough. The knowledge of God's love must sink from our heads into our hearts. Our only solid identity is as "His Beloved" — otherwise we create identities of self-protective intellectualism, or we wander, confused by the identities our culture tries to assign. We commit ourselves to pressing into the deep, deep love of God.

**Living in Humility**
Our dear friend George Miley defines humility as living according to the truth. The truth is that we are beautiful, because Jesus lives in us — and broken, because we continually leave his presence to follow our own ideas about what is best. To live in humility is to voluntarily embrace the truth about both, not in a masochistic manner but from a desire to be with Jesus.

**Praying and Obeying**
In Mark 3:13, Jesus calls to himself those he wants, that they might be with him and be sent out on mission. Prayer — being with Jesus — is the first thing. Obedience — going where he sends, whenever, wherever, with whomever — is the second. Life with Jesus is simple. It is not easy. And we are not bored.

**Desiring Unity As Much As Jesus Does**
Before he goes to the cross, Jesus prays his longest recorded prayer — and he prays for unity. Not for doctrinal correctness, not for effective evangelism, not for service to the poor — for unity, first among his disciples, then among all who would believe. The bar he sets is the love of the Trinity. We strive to desire unity as much as Jesus does, and to desire it as strongly.

**Honoring the Jews**
Both Catholic and Protestant streams have been guilty of hostility toward the Jewish people — from outright violence to subtler forms of replacement theology. This gives CTR the opportunity to stand side by side before Jewish brothers and sisters and repent together. We also rejoice in the re-emergence of an authentic Messianic Jewish stream in the Body of Christ — a river that went underground in the third and fourth centuries and has emerged again, with profound implications for all of us.

**Longing for the Return of Jesus**
We fan the flames of longing for Jesus' return — not as escapism, but as love. Every Bridge Prayer ends with the cry of the Bride:

*The Spirit and the Bride say, "Come!"*
*Even so, come quickly, Lord Jesus.*

**The Beauty of the Mystery of the Trinity**
Peter Kreeft has helpfully distinguished between problems, which are given to men to solve, and mysteries, which are given to men to draw forth worship. The Holy Trinity is a mystery. Our friend George Miley writes: each person of the Trinity relates to the others in harmony, submission, and honor — agape love. There is no dishonor, competition, jealousy, or unkindness among the Trinity. The love of each person of the Trinity finds perfection in honoring the others. For us, this is not a doctrine to master. It is a mystery to gaze upon in awe.

%%SECTION: WHAT WE ARE NOT%%
### What We Renounce

CTR's beliefs are not only affirmations. They include renunciations — specific forms of hostility that the community has named and committed to leaving behind.

Every member who makes the Statement of Personal Devotion renounces:

- Hostility toward God and toward all people
- Anti-Semitism
- Anti-Catholicism
- Anti-Protestantism
- Contempt toward all whom Christ has accepted

These are not polite gestures. They are specific, because the hostilities are specific. The wounds between traditions have real names and real histories. Healing them requires naming them.
