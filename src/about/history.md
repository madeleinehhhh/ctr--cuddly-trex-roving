### Our History

God has been faithful through more than twenty-five years of stumbling forward.

This is not a story of a community that had a plan and executed it. It is a story of a husband and wife who said yes to something they didn't fully understand, and found that God had been preparing the ground long before they arrived.

---

%%SECTION: 1999 — THE STIRRINGS%%
### 1999: Stirrings

In the late 1990s, Thomas and Amy Cogdell were being stirred by the Holy Spirit — each in their own way.

For Amy, the stirring was unexpected and unwelcome at first. As a happy member of Hope Chapel, a non-denominational church in Austin, she found her heart suddenly confronted with the pain of God's heart regarding the division of the Body of Christ — particularly the Catholic-Protestant divide. She hadn't gone looking for this. But when it came, she welcomed it as an intimate gift from God. By 1999 she was attending morning prayer, daily mass, and weekend services at the local Hispanic Catholic church down the street — and serving as a youth director there. She was a Protestant, fully at home in a Catholic parish, meeting God.

Thomas was mystified — but he trusted her ability to hear God's voice.

For Thomas, the stirring came on February 14, 1999, while walking in East Austin near the Holly Street Power Plant. Suddenly he heard the Holy Spirit whisper four words into his heart: *"Power Plant of Prayer."* He went home and drew the vision in his journal.

A few months later, he happened to be in Kansas City at a conference hosted by Metro Christian Fellowship. In the middle of the conference, Mike Bickle interrupted to announce they were starting a 24-hour house of prayer. The date was May 7, 1999. Thomas watched the first prayer team receive the key and go out — the beginning of what became the International House of Prayer (IHOP KC). He recognized immediately: *this is the same vision.*

In that same year, on their tenth wedding anniversary, Thomas and Amy traveled to Switzerland. In an ancient church in the small village of Romainmôtier, they heard the Lord open the door for Amy to officially become Catholic. It was on the same trip that they received the vision for what would become Wittenberg 2017.

%%SECTION: 1999–2003 — AUSTIN HOUSE OF PRAYER%%
### 1999–2003: Austin House of Prayer — Beginnings

Thomas began working toward a 24/7 prayer room in Austin. He found others pursuing the same vision and established two sets of prayer times — Thursday daytime sessions and a Friday night/Saturday morning night watch. This was the beginning of AHOP, the Austin House of Prayer.

Amy joined the Roman Catholic Church in 2001. Thomas fully supported this, understanding that God was asking them to live out reconciliation in the context of their own Protestant-Catholic marriage — before they could offer it to anyone else. Amy's decision was blessed by Dan Davis, the founder of Hope Chapel; by Fr. Larry Mattingly, her Catholic priest; and by both of their families.

%%SECTION: 2003–2012 — A NEW DIRECTION%%
### 2003–2012: A New Direction

Amy and Thomas were walking parallel paths. He was working hard to build AHOP. Her heart was being drawn into liturgy, silence, and contemplative prayer — life she did not find in the intense Harp and Bowl prayer model AHOP was built on.

The tension came to a crisis in 2003. Thomas came to his senses and realized he was damaging Amy by trying to force her participation in a ministry that was not what God had called her to. He repented. Together they asked: *what next?*

The answer surprised them. AHOP was not to be ended — but it had to change. If reconciliation was the core calling of their marriage, reconciliation had to be at the heart of AHOP.

Shortly afterward, Thomas received a vision for bottled water as an economic engine for the house of prayer. Cielo was born. Austin House of Prayer, Inc. was formally incorporated as a Texas non-profit in August 2004. AHOP rented a small building at the corner of MLK and Chicon in Austin — a prayer room and a small Cielo warehouse, side by side.

In 2006, AHOP moved to the corner of Real Street and Alexander Avenue, where — together with partners including Doxology, CS Lewis Hall, Regina Mater, and Imagine Art — the Seabrook Center was established, with the prayer room at its heart.

Throughout this season, AHOP worked alongside others laboring in the same vineyard: Trey Kent, Justin Christopher, Steve Hawthorne, David and Bethany Martin, Gregg Barnes, and many others. In some cases, AHOP served as a bridge connecting these leaders and their initiatives to the Catholic church, and vice versa. The work of reconciliation, it turned out, was not just a theology — it was a daily practice.

Key staff members through these years — Felipe Adams, Michael and Joelene Michel, Matt and Moriah LaCour — raised their own support to serve the body of Christ in the city.

%%SECTION: 2012–2017 — FOUNDATIONS%%
### 2012–2017: Christ the Reconciler — Foundations

2012 was a year of testing. The community had a desire to purchase property and thought they had a good strategy — joining with Imagine Art to buy land on Springdale Road. God put up a clear stop sign. When it became clear in October 2012 that the Wittenberg 2017 initiative was going to move forward, all ideas of purchasing property were set aside.

God has a sense of humor. As soon as they laid down the dream of purchasing land — they purchased land.

The Holy Spirit led Amy to 16.444 acres in Elgin, Texas, seven minutes south of town, thirty minutes east of Austin. The land had a large, unfinished house on it. It was purchased in 2013. This became Christ the Reconciler.

Fr. Peter Hocken, a Catholic priest from Austria who would become a significant friend and collaborator, visited the Elgin property shortly after it was acquired.

In August 2014, the finish-out of the house was completed and the community moved in — the Cogdell family, AHOP, and Cielo all together. Regular retreats began. Visitors came from around Texas, the US, and from overseas. Later in 2014, the ministry officially changed its name from Austin House of Prayer to Christ the Reconciler. The seeds of that change had been planted in 2004 — it took ten years to walk it out in God's timing.

The years 2012–2017 were also years of intense involvement in the Wittenberg 2017 initiative — a multi-year ecumenical project marking the 500th anniversary of the Reformation, with gatherings in Volkenroda (2013), Trento (2014), Rome (2015), and culminating in Wittenberg, Germany in November 2017. Thomas and Amy gave themselves wholeheartedly to this project, holding off further development of the CTR community to do so. %%SECTION: 2018 — YEAR OF REST%%
### 2018: A Year of Rest

After the culmination of Wittenberg 2017, it was clear that Thomas and Amy needed a sabbatical. But it was not a passive rest. It was a year of active listening — of discerning together what God was saying about the future of Christ the Reconciler.

The question they heard clearly was this: *What would it mean for a community to live out John 17?*

They explored it through a series of circle-preaching retreats on John 13–17, listening to each other and to the Lord about what he had in mind for CTR.

---

%%SECTION: 2019–PRESENT — LIVING OUT JOHN 17%%
### 2019–Present: Living Out John 17

2019 marked the beginning of a new season. With some trepidation, the community said yes to the high calling of striving toward John 17 as a community of Catholics, Protestants, and others from across the Body of Christ.

The first concrete step was the Statement of Personal Devotion — a personal rule of life for anyone who wants to take Jesus' prayer seriously in their own life, wherever they are.

Since then, the community has continued to develop its common life and its vision for the land. Phase 2 of the physical development — a 9,600 sq ft warehouse for Cielo — is complete. Phase 3, which will add the first residential houses and a prayer chapel, is planned for 2027.

The community now has members not just in Elgin and Austin but across the US and in Germany. By God's grace, CTR has been able to impact, face to face, people from 54 nations — more than 25% of the nations on earth.

How faithful God has been through the years and decades. The community walks in immaturity, partial revelation, and sinfulness. He loves them with faithfulness and patient tenderness. There is no one like our God.
