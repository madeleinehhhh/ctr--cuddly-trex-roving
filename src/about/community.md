### The Community

Christ the Reconciler is a community of lay followers of Jesus — Protestants, Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, Pentecostals, and Jews — living out John 17 together in Elgin, Texas, with members across the US and in Germany.

We are not a religious order. We are not a denomination. We are ordinary Christians who have said yes to an extraordinary calling: to live, work, and pray together across real theological difference, and to let that life be a visible witness to the unity Jesus prayed for.

---

%%SECTION: THE PEOPLE%%
### The People

CTR is made up of residential and non-residential members at different levels of commitment and participation.

**Residential members** live on the land in Elgin, sharing daily prayer, meals, and work. This is the heart of the common life — the place where the practices are most fully embodied and the reconciliation is most concretely tested.

**Non-residential members** are committed to the Rule of Life and the common life from a distance, rooted in their own parishes, churches, and synagogues, returning to Elgin regularly. CTR has non-residential members in other parts of the US and in Germany.

**Friends, supporters, and distant participants** follow along, pray the Bridge Prayer, listen to the teachings, and support the work. This is not a lesser form of participation. It is essential to the mission.

The community includes diverse ages, backgrounds, lived experiences, and political persuasions. What holds it together is not agreement on everything — it is a shared commitment to the prayer of Jesus in John 17, and a shared willingness to practice reconciliation rather than just talk about it.

%%SECTION: THE FOUNDERS%%
### The Founders

Thomas and Amy Cogdell founded Christ the Reconciler — though they would be the first to say that God founded it and they mostly tried to keep up.

Thomas is Protestant. Amy is Catholic. Their marriage has been, from the beginning, a living experiment in the reconciliation they preach. They have not had to imagine what it looks like to remain in genuine relationship across genuine theological difference. They have had to do it — at the dinner table, in how they pray, in how they raise their children, in the thousand small negotiations of a shared life.

That marriage is not a detail in the history of CTR. It is the origin of its charism. The grace that operates between them is the same grace that CTR offers to the wider Church — scaled up, extended outward, offered to anyone who wants it.

Thomas and Amy have been walking this road since 1999. They moved onto the Elgin land in 2014. They are still here.

%%SECTION: THE LAND%%
### The Land

*[Visual: aerial or ground-level photo of the Elgin property]*

Sixteen acres, seven minutes south of Elgin, thirty minutes east of Austin.

The land was acquired in 2013 — unexpectedly, as most of God's best gifts are. The Holy Spirit led Amy to it. The community closed on the property on November 21, 2013.

The vision for the land is simple and ambitious: to become a visible and visitable example of lived reconciliation. Not a retreat center. Not a conference facility. A place where reconciliation is the actual daily reality, and where anyone can come and see it.

%%SECTION: WHAT EXISTS NOW%%
### What's Here Now

**The Ministry Center**
The original house on the property was finished out in 2014 and became the hub of CTR's community life. It is where the community gathers for daily prayer, shares meals, hosts retreats, holds meetings, and welcomes guests. It is home.

**The Cielo Warehouse**
*[Visual: exterior of warehouse, Cielo branding]*

Cielo is CTR's bottled water company — and it is the economic engine of the community. In 2017, a 9,600 sq ft warehouse was built on the property to house the Cielo operation. In 2026, the manufacturing portion of the business was moved into the warehouse and significantly upgraded.

Cielo is not a side project or a fundraising mechanism. It is a genuine business whose purpose is to sustain the mission. It is also a concrete expression of the practice of work — the daily, unglamorous, necessary labor that makes common life possible.

%%SECTION: WHAT'S COMING%%
### What's Coming: Phase 3

*[Visual: site plan / concept renderings]*

Phase 3 — planned for 2027 — will add the first set of ten residential houses and a prayer chapel, along with shared community spaces, trails, and infrastructure.

The design is intentional in every detail:

**The chapel** sits at the center of the residential area, with an open lawn view axis leading to it. Everything orients toward prayer. Benches face the chapel. The architecture is doing theology.

**The houses** will be the first purpose-built residential spaces for community members — making it possible for more people to live the common life fully.

**Shared spaces** will include a community garden with raised beds, a lap pool, shaded pickleball courts, a maker space, a playground, and passive gathering areas with fire pits. These are not amenities. They are the infrastructure of a life shared together.

**Trails** will meander through the existing vegetation, connecting the residential area to Cielo and to secluded spaces for retreat and contemplation.

**A secluded retreat and meditation structure** will offer a quiet place set apart from the common activity — a reminder that the contemplative and the active are not opposites but partners.

**The community cemetery** is also planned. CTR is not a program with an end date. People are planning to be buried here. This community intends to last.

A naturalized bioswale at the entrance serves as a gateway crossing — a threshold between the outside world and the common life within. The land is meant to speak before anyone says a word.
